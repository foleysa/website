# The Double Squeeze: Why Your Total Landed Cost Just Changed Twice in One Month

*By John Foley | March 2026*

---

Procurement leaders are getting hit from two directions at once, and most haven't updated their math to reflect it.

From above: Section 122 tariffs landed a 10% levy on virtually all imports. Section 301 and Section 232 duties remain in full force. More than 20 states are suing to overturn the new tariffs, but the legal challenge won't resolve before the 150-day clock runs out on July 24.

From below: the Hormuz crisis rerouted global shipping around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 3,000 to 4,000 nautical miles per voyage. Maersk responded with emergency surcharges — $200 per TEU, $400 per FEU — on every long-haul route. Hapag-Lloyd, CMA CGM, and MSC followed within 48 hours. Container rates are up 40 to 60 percent since February. Insurance premiums for Gulf transit have increased more than 300 percent.

The result is a double squeeze on total landed cost that most procurement teams haven't fully quantified. Tariffs hit the duty line. Hormuz hit the freight line, the insurance line, the lead time line, and the inventory carrying cost line. Together, they've repriced the global supply chain in a matter of weeks.

---

## The Problem with FOB Thinking

Most procurement organizations still evaluate suppliers primarily on FOB price — the cost of goods at the point of origin. Freight, insurance, duties, inventory carrying costs, and lead time buffers are treated as separate line items managed by separate teams.

That mental model was already imperfect. Now it's dangerous.

A supplier in Southeast Asia that was 15 percent cheaper on an FOB basis may now be more expensive on a total landed cost basis once you factor in the new tariff schedule, the rerouted freight costs, the extended transit time, and the additional safety stock required to cover a 44-day ocean transit instead of a 30-day one.

The companies that figure this out first will reallocate volume before their competitors do. The companies that don't will spend the next six months wondering why their margins are eroding.

---

## Two Pressures, One Planning Horizon

What makes this moment unusual is that both pressures arrived within the same month and both have defined timelines.

The tariff pressure has a hard deadline. Section 122 authority includes a 150-day statutory limit. Unless renewed or replaced with different authority, these tariffs expire on July 24, 2026. That date is your planning horizon — not because the tariffs will necessarily disappear, but because the uncertainty around them demands scenario planning.

The freight pressure has no defined end. Nobody knows when the Strait of Hormuz reopens. Analysts have modeled scenarios from weeks to years. The rerouting costs, capacity constraints, and port congestion will persist for as long as the strait remains commercially unnavigable — and the secondary effects (diesel price spikes, container shortages, port congestion at Cape Town and Singapore) will persist even longer.

Smart procurement leaders are planning for both simultaneously.

---

## Four Actions Before July 24

**Remodel total landed cost.** This is the first and most important step. Take your top 20 suppliers by spend and rerun the total landed cost calculation with current freight rates, current tariff schedules, current insurance premiums, and current lead times. For many categories, the ranking of suppliers by true cost has changed. The answers you got in January are wrong today.

**Audit every IEEPA-era contract.** Contracts negotiated under the previous tariff regime carry assumptions about duty rates and freight costs that no longer hold. Review price adjustment clauses and duty pass-through terms. If your contract doesn't explicitly address tariff changes or freight surcharges, you — not your supplier — are absorbing the increase. Force majeure language matters here too: suppliers may attempt to invoke it for Hormuz-related delays even if the contract was signed after the crisis began.

**Accelerate dual-sourcing.** The combined impact of tariffs and rerouting costs has changed the nearshoring equation overnight. Mexico, Central America, and Eastern Europe were already gaining share in sourcing strategies. The double squeeze makes them dramatically more competitive on a total landed cost basis. Run the comparison again with today's numbers. What didn't pencil out in January almost certainly pencils out now.

**Use the 150-day window.** Build two scenarios: one where Section 122 tariffs expire on July 24, and one where they're renewed or replaced. Make sourcing decisions that perform under both scenarios. This means prioritizing flexibility — shorter contract terms, index-based pricing, options on nearshore capacity — over locking in long-term commitments in an environment where the rules may change again in four months.

---

## The Bottom Line

Your total landed cost changed twice in one month. Once from tariffs, once from Hormuz. If you haven't rerun the math, every sourcing decision you make between now and July is based on the wrong numbers.

The double squeeze is compressing margins from both directions simultaneously. The procurement teams that act on this now — remodeling costs, auditing contracts, accelerating nearshoring, scenario-planning around the 150-day window — will come out of this period with a structural advantage.

The ones that wait will spend the next quarter explaining to their CFO why costs are up and nobody saw it coming.
