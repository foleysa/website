Supply Chain Resilience in the Hormuz Era: A Practical Framework for Mid-Market Companies

By John Foley  |  March 2026

Two weeks ago, the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed. If your company has any exposure to Gulf-sourced materials, energy, or suppliers — and you haven't already started war-gaming your response — you're behind.

Let me be blunt: this is the largest supply disruption in modern history. Not a stress test. Not a drill. The real thing.

What Just Happened

On February 28, the escalating US-Israeli conflict with Iran shut down transit through the Strait of Hormuz. Twenty percent of global oil supply — gone from the market overnight. WTI crude surged from roughly $70 to $96-98 per barrel in days, and that number isn't coming back down soon. Gulf state oil production has dropped more than 10 million barrels per day.

On March 4, QatarEnergy declared force majeure on all LNG shipments. That's not a partial curtailment — that's a full stop on liquefied natural gas from the world's largest LNG exporter. Bahrain's Alba aluminum smelter, one of the world's largest, cut production by 19%. These are dominoes, and they're still falling.

If you're running a mid-market company and think this is someone else's problem, think again. Energy costs feed into everything: transportation, manufacturing, raw materials, packaging, logistics. Even if you don't source a single component from the Gulf, your suppliers might. Their suppliers almost certainly do.

Why Mid-Market Companies Are Most Exposed

Fortune 100 companies have entire risk management teams, eight-figure consulting engagements, and diversified global supply networks built over decades. They'll absorb this hit. It'll hurt, but they'll survive.

Mid-market companies — $100M to $2B in revenue — typically don't have that infrastructure. Most have lean procurement teams running hard just to keep operations moving. Supply chain risk management, if it exists at all, is a spreadsheet someone updates quarterly.

I've seen this from both sides. During my time at Accenture, I helped large enterprises build the kind of resilience frameworks that let them weather disruptions. As a procurement leader inside Fortune 50 operations, I watched those frameworks get tested — and I saw which ones held and which ones didn't. The difference was never budget. It was preparation and speed of response.

Mid-market companies can build resilience. But they can't do it the way the big shops do — with 18-month transformation programs and seven-figure strategy decks. They need a framework they can execute in weeks, not quarters.

A Practical Framework: Four Steps to Resilience

Here's what I'd tell any mid-market CEO or COO right now. This isn't theory. This is what works.

1. Map Your Exposure — Ruthlessly and Quickly

You need to know, within the next 72 hours, exactly where your supply chain touches the Gulf. Not just Tier 1 suppliers — go at least to Tier 2, and ideally Tier 3.

Start with energy. What percentage of your energy costs are tied to natural gas or petroleum products that originate from Gulf states? Then move to raw materials. Petrochemicals, aluminum, specialty chemicals, plastics feedstocks — all of these have significant Gulf exposure.

Don't overthink this. You're not building a perfect model. You're building a heat map. Red means "this breaks us if it goes away for 90 days." Yellow means "painful but survivable." Green means "minimal exposure." Anything you can't categorize goes in red until proven otherwise.

From my Air Force days: in operational planning, imperfect intelligence acted on quickly beats perfect intelligence that arrives too late. Same principle applies here.

2. Identify Alternatives — Before You Need Them

For every red-zone dependency, you need at least one alternative source identified. Not contracted — identified. Know who they are, where they are, what their capacity looks like, and what the lead time is to switch.

This is where most companies stall. They look at their primary supplier, see that switching costs are high, and decide to wait it out. That's a bet. And right now, it's a bad one. Nobody knows when Hormuz reopens. Could be weeks. Could be months. Could be longer.

Alternative sourcing doesn't mean you abandon existing relationships. It means you have options. Options are leverage, and leverage is survival.

3. Build Buffer Stock Strategies

If you haven't already adjusted your inventory posture, you're competing with everyone else who just realized they should. Spot markets for Gulf-dependent materials are already moving. Prices will continue to climb as the disruption persists.

Determine your critical materials — the ones that shut down your operations if they run out. Calculate your current days-of-supply. Then make a hard decision about how much buffer you need to carry and what that costs versus the cost of a production shutdown.

This isn't just about physical inventory. It's about financial hedging, contract flexibility, and having pre-negotiated agreements with alternative suppliers that you can activate on short notice.

4. Stress-Test Your Supplier Network

Take your top 20 suppliers by spend and ask them a simple question: "What is your exposure to Gulf-sourced energy and materials, and what is your contingency plan?"

You'll learn two things very quickly. First, which suppliers have actually thought about this. Second, which ones are about to become your biggest risk. The suppliers who can't answer that question coherently are the ones most likely to fail you in the next 60-90 days.

I ran supplier stress tests during my Accenture years for companies navigating far less severe disruptions than this. The pattern is always the same: about 30% of suppliers have real contingency plans, about 40% have something on paper that won't survive contact with reality, and about 30% have nothing. Knowing which bucket your suppliers fall into is the first step toward managing the risk.

Speed Is the Variable That Matters

Every framework is only as good as its execution speed. The companies that will come through this disruption strongest are the ones acting now — not the ones commissioning a six-month supply chain review.

Mid-market companies actually have an advantage here: shorter decision cycles, less bureaucracy, more direct access between leadership and operations. Use that. A mid-market procurement team that moves fast will outperform an enterprise team stuck in approval chains.

But speed requires focus. You need someone who has done this before, who knows what to look for, and who can drive a resilience assessment to actionable recommendations in weeks, not months.

The Bottom Line

The Hormuz closure isn't a temporary disruption — it's a structural shift that will reshape supply chains for years. Companies that build resilience now will have a competitive advantage that compounds over time. Companies that wait will be playing catch-up from a position of weakness.
