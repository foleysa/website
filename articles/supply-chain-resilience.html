The 4-Step Framework
01 Map Your Exposure

A 72-hour exercise. Identify where your supply chain touches the Gulf across Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 suppliers. Start with energy, then trace raw materials: petrochemicals, aluminum, specialty chemicals, plastics feedstocks.

Build a heat map. Anything you cannot categorize goes red until proven otherwise. Imperfect intelligence acted on quickly beats perfect intelligence that arrives too late.

02 Identify Alternatives

For every red-zone dependency, identify at least one alternative source. Not contracted yet — just identified. Know who they are, where they operate, their capacity, and the lead time to switch.

Nobody knows when Hormuz reopens: weeks, months, or longer. Options equal leverage, and right now, leverage equals survival.

03 Build Buffer Stock

Adjust inventory posture now. If you have not started, you are already competing with everyone who just realized they should. Spot markets for Gulf-dependent materials are moving fast.

Go beyond physical inventory: financial hedging, contract flexibility, and pre-negotiated backup agreements all count as buffer.

04 Stress-Test Your Suppliers

Take your top 20 suppliers by spend. Ask one question: What is your exposure to Gulf-sourced energy and materials, and what is your contingency plan?

Framework in Action
Example: Mid-Market Industrial Coatings Manufacturer
$800M revenue. 14 production facilities across North America. Procurement team of 6. No dedicated risk function. Supply chain managed through quarterly business reviews and a category-spend spreadsheet.
01
Map exposure. 72-hour sprint reveals: resin feedstock (petrochemical-derived) sources back to two Gulf-region refineries at Tier 2. Natural gas — 38% of energy cost — priced off benchmarks now spiking. Titanium dioxide pigment supplier uses Gulf-shipped bauxite. Result: 3 categories flagged RED, 5 flagged YELLOW, the rest GREEN.
02
Identify alternatives. For resin feedstock: two domestic petrochemical producers identified with available capacity and 4-6 week qualification lead time. For TiO2 pigment: one Australian-sourced alternative, higher unit cost but no Gulf dependency. Switching costs look high — but a 90-day production shutdown costs more.
03
Build buffer. Current days-of-supply on resin feedstock: 18 days. Target: 45 days. Spot purchase placed immediately for 60-day bridge stock. Finance team pre-approves a natural gas hedge at current rates. Backup supply agreement drafted with domestic resin producer — not executed yet, but ready.
04
Stress-test suppliers. Top 20 suppliers surveyed. Results: 5 have real contingency plans with alternative sourcing already activated. 9 have documented plans that have never been tested. 6 had no answer. The 6 with no answer include two sole-source suppliers — those become the priority escalation.
Total elapsed time: 8 days. Total out-of-pocket cost: the spot purchase and staff time. The company now has a risk posture that would have taken a Big Four engagement 12 weeks and six figures to produce.
