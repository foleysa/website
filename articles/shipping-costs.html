The Maersk Surcharge Is Just the Start: How to Get Ahead of Structural Shipping Cost Increases

By John Foley  |  March 2026

Last week, Maersk announced an Emergency Bunker Surcharge effective March 25 — $200 per TEU, $400 per FEU on all long-haul routes. Reefer containers get hit even harder: $300 and $600 respectively. If you're in procurement and this caught you off guard, we need to talk.

Because this isn't a one-carrier problem. And it's not going away in sixty days.

The Surcharge Is a Symptom

Within weeks, expect the other major carriers to follow Maersk's lead. They always do. When the market leader moves on surcharges, it gives everyone else cover to do the same. MSC, CMA CGM, Hapag-Lloyd — they're all facing the same cost pressures, and none of them are going to eat it.

The root causes here aren't mysterious. They're geopolitical, they're structural, and they're compounding:

The Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed for commercial transit. Tensions in the Persian Gulf have made the risk calculus untenable for most carriers and their insurers.

The Suez Canal and Bab al-Mandab remain high-risk corridors. Houthi attacks on commercial shipping — which started as a nuisance in late 2023 — have become a sustained campaign. Insurance premiums for Red Sea transit have skyrocketed, and most carriers have simply stopped trying.

The result: Cape of Good Hope rerouting is now the default, not the exception. That adds 10-14 days to Asia-Europe routes and 7-10 days to Asia-East Coast US routes. More days at sea means more fuel burned, more vessel-days consumed, and fewer available slots in the rotation.

This is basic math. When you add thousands of nautical miles to the world's busiest trade lanes, costs go up and capacity goes down — simultaneously.

This Is Structural, Not Cyclical

Here's where I see too many procurement leaders getting it wrong. They're treating this like the post-COVID container crunch — a spike that'll correct itself once things "normalize." That thinking is dangerous.

There is no normalization path in the near term. The geopolitical conditions driving rerouting aren't resolving. If anything, they're intensifying. And unlike COVID-era port congestion, which was a demand-and-logistics problem that unwound as supply chains rebalanced, today's cost drivers are baked into the physical geography of global trade.

Yes, the industry is responding on the capacity side. Asian carriers like SITC and Wan Hai Lines are ordering billions of dollars in new vessels. The global orderbook sits at a record 11.8 million TEU. That's an enormous amount of steel in the pipeline.

But here's the catch: those ships won't hit the water for two to three years. Shipyards are booked. Construction timelines are what they are. And even when new capacity arrives, it has to be absorbed into networks that have been fundamentally reshaped by longer routes.

So the math for the next 24 to 36 months is straightforward: demand for vessel capacity stays elevated, supply is constrained, and the cost of moving a container from Point A to Point B is structurally higher than it was 18 months ago.

Plan accordingly.

What Procurement Leaders Should Do Right Now

I spent years at Accenture advising Fortune 500 companies on supply chain strategy, and before that I spent over two decades in the Air Force where logistics wasn't an academic exercise — it was mission-critical. In both worlds, the same principle holds: you don't wait for the situation to stabilize before you act. You act so you're in position when it does.

Here's what I'd be doing if I were sitting in a CPO's chair right now:

1. Renegotiate Freight Contracts Immediately

If your ocean freight contracts are up for renewal in the next six months, move the timeline up. Carriers are going to keep pushing rates higher. Locking in now — even at today's elevated rates — may look smart by Q4. If you're on spot market exposure, that's an even more urgent conversation.

2. Consolidate Shipments Ruthlessly

Every partial container is money left on the table. Work with your logistics team to consolidate loads, optimize container utilization, and reduce the number of individual shipments. This isn't glamorous work, but it's high-impact when per-container costs are climbing.

3. Evaluate Nearshoring — Seriously This Time

I've heard "we're looking at nearshoring" from procurement leaders for five years now. For most, it's stayed at the PowerPoint stage. The calculus has changed. When ocean freight adds 15-25% to your landed cost on a sustained basis, manufacturing in Mexico, Central America, or even domestic reshoring starts to pencil out differently. Run the numbers with today's freight reality, not 2022's.

4. Build Transport Diversification Into Your Sourcing Strategy

If your sourcing strategy doesn't account for how goods get to you, it's incomplete. Evaluate suppliers not just on unit cost but on total landed cost under multiple transport scenarios. A supplier that's 5% cheaper but entirely dependent on a single ocean lane through contested waters isn't actually cheaper — they're a risk.

5. Model Total Landed Cost Scenarios

Build three scenarios: current state, moderate escalation (another 15-20% freight increase over 12 months), and severe disruption (major lane closure or carrier capacity crunch). If your supply chain breaks under scenario two, you have work to do. If it breaks under scenario three, you need a contingency plan yesterday.

The Window Is Closing

The companies that will come through this in the strongest position are the ones treating it as what it is: a multi-year structural shift in the cost of global trade. Not a blip. Not a temporary surcharge that'll get rolled back once things "calm down."

I've been in enough operational environments — military and corporate — to know that the organizations that thrive under pressure are the ones that read the signals early and move decisively. The Maersk surcharge is a signal. The question is what you do with it.

The organizations still running 2024 freight assumptions in their 2026 budgets are going to get caught. The ones scenario-planning, renegotiating, and diversifying now will have options when their competitors don't.
